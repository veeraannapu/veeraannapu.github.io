What Pair Programming Actually Taught Me

VA
Veera Annapureddy
·
May 2025
7 min read

I started doing pair programming consistently in mid-2023. Before that, I'd pulled colleagues in for specific debugging sessions here and there — but deliberate, regular pairing as part of how the team actually works was new to me.

A couple of years in, it's changed how I think about writing software more than I expected. This is what I actually learned from doing it — not the theory, but what shifts when you pair regularly and do it with intention.

How We Actually Do It

We pair remotely using Tuple. If you haven't used it, it's a tool built specifically for remote pair programming — low latency, proper remote control so both people can actually type, and a fast connection flow that removes most of the friction from starting a session.

🔗 Why Tuple works well for remote pairing

Purpose-built for pairing — the remote control is genuinely snappy, meaning the other person can reach in and type without waiting. That small thing changes the dynamic significantly. Both people stay active in the session rather than one watching and one driving the whole time.

Sessions run 60–90 minutes with a break. We switch driver and navigator every 20–30 minutes. Sometimes we do shorter focused sessions — working through an unfamiliar part of the codebase, a tricky debugging problem, or a design discussion before writing anything.

The Roles

The driver holds the keyboard and writes the code. The navigator watches, thinks ahead, catches mistakes, and asks questions. You switch regularly.

The thing I didn't understand at first: the navigator role is where most of the learning happens. When you're driving, you're executing — in the details, making the thing work. When you're navigating, you have to follow someone else's reasoning in real time without controlling it. That's harder than it sounds, and it builds a different kind of understanding than writing the code yourself does.

When It Works and When to Skip It

✅ Good fit for pairing
  • Complex logic with many edge cases
  • Onboarding someone to an unfamiliar codebase
  • Debugging something neither person fully understands
  • Designing a new API or data model
  • Code touching security, payments, or data integrity
  • Anything where getting it wrong has real consequences
⬜ Skip pairing here
  • Mechanical tasks — boilerplate, config, formatting
  • Research and exploration — let one person dig first
  • Writing docs or tests for finished features
  • Work that needs deep single-person focus
  • Routine dependency updates

Pairing is a tool, not a religion. Applying it to everything is as wrong as applying it to nothing. The value comes from being deliberate about when to use it.

What I Actually Learned

1
You learn how other people think, not just what they know
Reading someone's code tells you what they decided. Watching them write code tells you how they decide. You see the alternatives they considered and dropped. You see how they navigate uncertainty. That mental model — their decision process — is what you can't get from a code review or a doc.
2
You find out what you actually know vs what you think you know
There's a specific uncomfortable moment when you're explaining something mid-session and realise mid-sentence that you don't understand it as well as you thought. That discomfort is useful. You can't fake understanding when you have to explain it out loud in real time to someone else.
3
The navigator catches bugs the driver doesn't see
When you write code, you read it through the intention in your head — you see what you meant to write. A navigator has no such intention. They read what's actually there. The null check you meant to add. The off-by-one your mental model glossed over. These surface quickly when someone else is watching without your assumptions.
4
Design conversations happen earlier
Before you write a function together, you have to agree on what it does. That conversation — "what should this return?", "what are the failure cases?", "do we validate here or upstream?" — produces better design than one person deciding alone and another reviewing after the fact. The questions come when they're cheaper to answer.
5
Onboarding gets faster in a way documentation can't replicate
When someone new pairs with an experienced engineer from day one, they absorb context that would take months from docs alone. Not just the code — the unwritten rules. Why this service is structured the way it is. Which parts are fragile. Which patterns the team has moved away from. That knowledge transfers during pairing in hours, not weeks.

What Makes It Go Wrong

The navigator goes passive

The driver moves fast, and the navigator starts just watching. This usually happens when the navigator feels unsure and doesn't want to slow things down by asking questions. That's the wrong instinct. If you're navigating and you've stopped actively following what's happening — slow down, say so, ask. That's literally the job.

One person dominates the whole session

If one person drives for two hours and the other hasn't typed a line, it's not pairing — it's supervised coding. The rotation matters. Switching is what keeps both people engaged and prevents one person from burning out.

Treating it as a substitute for documentation

Pairing transfers knowledge between two people in the room right now. It does nothing for the person who joins in six months. If something was decided during a session that isn't obvious from the code — write it in a comment before the session ends. Pairing is for learning and building. Documentation is for remembering.

The rule that sticks

If we made a decision during pairing that a future reader of this code wouldn't understand from the code alone — it goes into a comment before we close the session.

What I'd Tell Someone Starting Out

When I first started pairing regularly, I spent energy worrying about looking slow or asking obvious questions. That was the wrong thing to focus on. The questions that feel obvious to you are often the ones that expose gaps in how the problem has been framed — or gaps in your own understanding that are worth surfacing early.

If you're the more experienced person in the pair: resist the urge to take over when the other person is struggling. The struggle is where the learning is. Your job is to keep them productive in it, not rescue them from it. Ask questions before giving answers. Let them drive more than feels comfortable.

The best sessions I've had didn't feel like teaching. They felt like two people genuinely thinking through something together — even when there was a significant experience gap. That's the version worth aiming for.
